## Introduction to Discrete Mathematics

Discrete mathematics is the bedrock of computer science. While continuous mathematics (like calculus) models the analog world, discrete math models the digital world. It deals with structures that are fundamentally countable, distinct, and separable.

### 1. Logic and Boolean Algebra
Logic is the study of formal reasoning. In CSE, this manifests as:
- **Propositional Logic**: Using logical connectives (AND, OR, NOT, XOR) to build complex truth tables.
- **Predicate Logic**: Using quantifiers (∀, ∃) to express statements about sets of objects.
- **Digital Logic Design**: Using logic gates (NAND, NOR) to build the hardware circuits that power CPUs.

### 2. Set Theory and Relations
Sets are the most basic mathematical structure.
- **Operations**: Union (∪), Intersection (∩), and Complement (A').
- **Relations**: Essential for **Relational Databases (SQL)**. Understanding properties like reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity is key to understanding joins and indexing.
- **Functions**: Understanding injectivity (one-to-one) and surjectivity (onto) is critical for hashing algorithms and type theory.

### 3. Graph Theory
Perhaps the most applied area of discrete math in programming.
- **Nodes and Edges**: Modeling social networks, internet routing, and dependency graphs.
- **Trees**: The hierarchical structure used in file systems, DOM trees, and XML/JSON parsing.
- **Algorithms**: Shortest path (Dijkstra's), Minimum Spanning Trees (Kruskal's), and Network Flow.

### 4. Combinatorics and Complexity
The art of counting.
- **Permutations and Combinations**: Calculating the total possible states of a system.
- **Pigeonhole Principle**: A simple but powerful tool for proving limits in algorithms.
- **Recurrence Relations**: Used to solve for the time complexity (Big O) of recursive algorithms like Merge Sort.
